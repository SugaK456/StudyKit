Scenario Description

A major Italian coffee brand is evaluating the environmental performance of its single-serve aluminium coffee capsule system. The goal of this exercise is to perform a cradle-to-grave LCA of the system, from the production of the capsule and its contents to end-of-life treatment.

The capsule is produced in a facility in Germany (DE). It is made of aluminium (3.2 g) and a polypropylene lid (0.5 g), sealed with a paper filter (0.3 g). The assembly of one capsule requires 0.8 Wh of medium-voltage electricity. The detailed bill of materials is reported in Table 1.

Each capsule contains 5.5 g of roasted and ground coffee. Coffee is grown and processed in Colombia, and the unitary impacts of 1 kg of roasted coffee (including cultivation, processing and export preparation) are reported in Table 2. The coffee is then transported from Colombia to Hamburg by container ship (10,450 km), and subsequently transported by small lorry (680 km) to the filling plant in Italy (IT).

The filled capsules are packed into cardboard boxes (10 capsules per box, box weight 48 g) at the Italian plant. Cardboard box production requires 0.35 kWh of medium-voltage electricity per kg of cardboard. The packaged product is then transported by small lorry (220 km) to a distribution centre and subsequently to retailers.

During the use phase, the consumer brews one capsule per use using a coffee machine. The machine consumes 0.033 kWh of electricity (low voltage) per brewing cycle, and 200 ml of tap water (at consumer level, from surface water – IT).

☛ Functional unit: the preparation and consumption of 1 cup of coffee (one capsule brewing cycle). The system boundary covers all stages from raw material extraction to end-of-life.

At end-of-life, two scenarios must be modelled. In the reference scenario, the used capsule (containing residual coffee grounds) is disposed of as municipal solid waste and sent to incineration with energy recovery. The unitary impacts of incineration of the capsule (aluminium + PP lid + paper filter + coffee grounds) are reported in Table 3. In the alternative scenario, the capsule is separately collected and sent to aluminium recycling, using the system expansion approach: the recycled aluminium displaces primary aluminium production. Consider that 85% of the aluminium is recovered during recycling, and the collection and sorting process requires 0.04 kWh/capsule of medium-voltage electricity.

The considered impact categories are "Climate change" and "Resource use, fossils" of the Impact assessment method EF 3.0 Method (adapted).

Refer to Tables 4 and 5 for indications on elementary flows and providers that must be used in the modelling.

The battery has a service life of 3 years (one replacement during the scooter's lifetime). Energy consumption during use is 0.028 kWh per km (low-voltage electricity, Netherlands grid).

Scenario Description

A furniture retailer is evaluating the environmental profile of a flat-pack bookshelf available in two material variants: one made from solid spruce wood and one made from medium-density fibreboard (MDF). The goal of this exercise is to perform a comparative LCA of the two product variants.

The system boundary includes raw material extraction, manufacturing (cutting, milling, surface treatment), packaging, transport to a retail warehouse in Sweden (SE), and end-of-life. The assembly by the consumer is excluded from the system boundary.

Both bookshelf variants have the same dimensions and load-bearing capacity. The solid wood bookshelf is manufactured in a carpentry in Austria (AT). The final bookshelf requires 18 kg of finished spruce boards. Due to cutting and planing, 25% of the raw timber input is lost as wood waste, which is incinerated with energy recovery at the carpentry site. The manufacturing process consumes 2.4 kWh of medium-voltage electricity and 18 MJ of heat produced by burning desulphurised natural gas (net calorific value 44.5 MJ/kg; combustion releases 2.6 kg CO₂ per kg of gas). The surface of the solid wood bookshelf is treated with 0.6 kg of water-based lacquer; unitary impacts of the lacquer are given in Table 2.

The MDF bookshelf is produced in a factory in Poland (PL) using 22 kg of MDF panels. Manufacturing requires 3.8 kWh of medium-voltage electricity and 25 MJ of heat (same natural gas assumptions as above). The MDF surface is treated with 0.45 kg of melamine resin coating; the unitary impacts of melamine resin coating are given in Table 2.

Both products are packaged in corrugated cardboard (2.4 kg per bookshelf). Both bookshelves are transported by small lorry to the Swedish warehouse: the solid wood variant travels 1,640 km and the MDF variant travels 950 km. Both weigh approximately 24 kg (including packaging) when shipped.

☛ Functional unit: one bookshelf providing 10 years of storage service. Adjust the model if the service lives of the two variants differ (solid wood: 15 years; MDF: 8 years).

At end-of-life, the bookshelf is assumed to be collected as bulky waste and incinerated (with energy recovery). The unitary impacts of end-of-life incineration are reported in Table 3.
